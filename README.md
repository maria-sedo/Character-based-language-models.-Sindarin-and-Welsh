# Character-based language models: Sindarin and Welsh

Exercise 2 of the Natural Language Processing course. Master in Theoretical and Applied Linguistics (Universitat Pompeu Fabra).

J. R. R. Tolkien, author of the beloved fiction The Lord of The Rings, created a world rich in linguistic diversity. He used existing languages as a base for creating new ones, and the names of his characters follow the phonotactics of the languages. For example, Sindarin was based on Welsh. Studies suggest that, besides using real languages, Tolkien preferred certain letter combinations, which he used in names regardless of the patterns displayed by the languages (Baker-Smemoe et al., 2014; Brown et al., 2018). These combinations form the phonoprint, consistent phoneme patterns that characterize an author. Baker-Smemoe et al. (2014) identified Tolkien’s phonoprint as the sounds /b/, /r/, /n/, /l/, /a/, /æ/, /ɛ/, the onsets /br/, /gr/, /gl/, /dr/, and the codas /n/, /m/, /l/, /r/, /nt/ and /rn/.

Building on these studies, this project aimed to define and train two character-based language models, one for Sindarin and one for Welsh, to generate character and personal names (we cannot claim to generate only personal names, as Tolkien gave Sindarin names to different species). The main goal was to assess the quality of the models in generating names: first in the training language and then in the second language. We expected both models to generate plausible names for the training language, and hypothesized that the Sindarin model outputs might reflect Tolkien’s phonoprint. 

The two models defined are MLP (Multilayer Perceptron) language models that capture patterns from the input TXT files and generate new names. The models followed the character-level language model proposed by Karpathy (2022), with modifications. To test the transfer to the second language, two additional versions of the models were created. These copies received initial context from the second language (first three characters) to generate names with the saved trained models.

Our expectations were met: in general, both models generated plausible names for the language in which they were trained, and the Sindarin model outputs reflected Tolkien’s phonoprint. The losses achieved indicated slight overfitting, but the final training-validation gaps were acceptable given the small datasets. The generated names were not direct copies from the input but plausible new names of the languages that followed orthographic and phonotactic patterns. For example, Sindarin names frequently contained -ol-, -or-, -el-, and -nd-, while Welsh names showed patterns such as -wy- and ff-.

Regarding Tolkien’s phonoprint, frequencies of /b/, /r/, /n/, /l/, /a/, and codas /n/, /l/, /r/ and /rn/ were notable in the Sindarin names generated by the Sindarin model, while other patterns appeared rarely (onsets /br/, /gr/, /gl/ and /dr/, and codas /m/ and /nt/). These results support the phonoprint described by Baker-Smemoe et al. (2014) and illustrate how neural networks reproduce probabilistic patterns.

In the generated names for the second language, it could be seen that Sindarin and Welsh share certain phonotactic patterns, consistent with Tolkien’s use of Welsh as a base for Sindarin, but many of the names could be distinguished. Names produced by the Sindarin model with Welsh context appeared mostly Sindarin, whereas Welsh-model outputs with Sindarin context appeared Welsh. This suggests that the models reflect patterns learned during the training rather than those of the language providing the initial context.

Overall, Sindarin patterns were more recognisable than Welsh ones. Sindarin outputs were more regular, while Welsh outputs were more diverse and complex. This may reflect the difference between artificial languages like Sindarin and natural languages like Welsh (which show greater variation and irregularity). 

In this repository, the models created are provided along with the the TXT files with Sindarin and Welsh names used as input and the .pt files with the trained models. Sindarin names were collected from the dictionary Parf Edhellen (n.d.), and Welsh names were collected from the website Behind the Name (Campbell, 2025). The Sindarin file contains 328 names, and the Welsh file contains 314 names.
